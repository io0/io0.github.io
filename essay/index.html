I changed schools once a year for six years. From China to Montreal and Montreal to Toronto, I followed along while my parents figured out Western society for themselves. I had yet to learn a single social script when I was forced to learn many at once; my experience of humanity became quite stunning.

Know when to laugh and when to stop; learn French — now learn English; learn what pizza is because everyone seems to know it and if you don't then you must be dumb or strange. My heart rate spiked, I girded my loins. Witch trials could happen at a moment's notice.

Childhood created an immense inner life. One time when I was seven we had to write short stories. There were twenty or so stories about a boy named Sam looking for his puppy. Next to them, in monk-like handwriting, was about a bird named Springshine who flew around the world to change the seasons.

I was fascinated by the color chartreuse. I loved and collected things like that. It made me happy to learn they existed, I collected bits and pieces of text about politics, old things, churches, dragons, the Monkey King, the Yukon. I didn't know what they really were, but had a preverbal sense that these things were fantastic.

I truly loved the textures I could feel in my mind. I wrote short stories in a single Word document that grew to 102k words by the time I was 12.

I was nudged into science in high school. Everyone said the subject was challenging, and it seemed like biology might have answers about the mind. Action preceded love; only after a couple brutalistic readings of Campbell Biology did I see biology as fantastic.
