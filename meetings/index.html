Joel Simon

Entry from 2021-12-12 23:48

I met Joel today — it was cool. They had a human brain in a vat, over their fireplace. A real brain... that was once a real person. He didn't seem surprised by it at all, and when I asked how it got there, he answered, "well, we have a neuroscience professor in the house."

Joel's story is that he was working full-time, and he released Artbreeder as a side project. I remember what it looked like in the beginning, circa 2018. It generated a lot of strange, abnormal-looking creatures that weren't particularly aesthetic.

But now the art has become good— fantastical, imaginative. The project grew and went viral on TikTok. Someone on Youtube made a video about artbreeder that got 3 million views (this happened a couple months ago). So Artbreeder is now profitable enough to be his full-time thing.

It took a while. Artbreeder got started in 2018, and it really needed the GANs to get better before it started taking off 3 years later.

Contracting

Contracting is a great way to have a community. Molly is working on Moth Minds alone, so she took preemptive steps to make sure she doesn't go crazy. She is doing two part-time jobs: a writing role at Stripe Press with Tamara Winter and one other person, under Patrick Collison, and a design role at Primer making a blogging platform for the kids. She's doing one day on each contracting role every week, and spending the rest of her time on Moth Minds.

  • I thought this was really interesting
  • My mental model of work is that you should only be doing one thing, ever. Perhaps there's another statement, equally axiomatic: you have to be with people. If the second is not satisfied, then maybe you should trade off on the first?
    • there was a time at Google X when I was also doing a side project that took most of my time from 6 pm to 3 am, then I would show up to work and be pretty low-energy for the daytime. I thought that this was evidence that you shouldn't do multiple things at once, but perhaps there's something about doing a job that provides a lower-amplitude but consistent form of feedback that you don't get from independent projects
  • I asked if she would have trouble context-switching, but basically she said it's worth it.
    • It's also about knowing yourself. She has experimented with different modes of work before
    • She also thinks that the people you work with in contracting can also help you with your own thing. I believe this, esp since the contracting roles seem high-quality
  • The contracting roles are in a team (of contractors). She's working directly with 2 other people at Stripe and 1 other person at Primer.
    • I think pair-programming or the equivalent should be the default mode of working. There's this implicit assumption that people should be treated like separate processors in wait of a task, but I think it's the thing in between people that gets stuff done
      • See Sah and Sawhney, Larry and Sergey
